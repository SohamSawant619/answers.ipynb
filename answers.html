Q1.1. What is the approximate angular size of the galaxy cluster?¶

The angular size of a cluster refers to the apparent size of the cluster as observed from a particular vantage point, typically from Earth. It is measured as an angle and represents the extent of the cluster in the sky.¶

Here we have used the angular separation formula to find the angular size of the galaxy cluster by taking the angular separation of coordinates of galaxies at the extreme ends of the cluster by taking the minimum and maximum of Right Ascension(RA) at J2000.0 and Declination and J2000.0.

RA is analogous to longitude on the Earth's surface, but instead of measuring east and west, it measures eastward around the celestial equator and Dec is analogous to latitude on the Earth's surface, but instead of measuring north and south, it measures angular distance above or below the celestial equator.¶

To find the angular size we used this formula:-


$cos(θ) = sin(δ_1)*sin(δ_2) + cos(δ_1)*cos(δ_2)*cos(α_1 - α_2)$
Here, we have converted the values of RA and Dec from degrees to radians during the usage of this formula which could be used in calculation further.

Section 4 : Getting the Hubble's Constant¶

The Hubble constant, denoted by $H_0$ ​ , is a fundamental parameter in cosmology that describes the rate of expansion of the universe. It quantifies the current rate at which galaxies are receding from us due to the expansion of We can find Hubble constant using Hubble Lemaitre law which states that


$v = H_0*d$
where v is the recession velocity i.e velocity of galaxies moving away from the observer
d is the instantaneous distance between source and the observer space.
